Where the money is going

Your site is costing you patients every day

The average cosmetic dental patient is worth $4,000 a year. Each problem below is actively sending patients to a competitor who fixed it.

We check every practice against this list before reaching out. Three problems is normal. All six is common.
01

Not mobile optimised

55% of patients search on mobile. A site built for desktop delivers a broken, unreadable experience to more than half the people actively looking for you — before they've read a word. They leave and call someone else.

Half your trafficlost before they read a word
02

Loads too slowly

53% of mobile visitors abandon a page that takes over 3 seconds. The average dental site loads in 6–8 seconds. That means more than half the traffic you're paying to reach — through Google, word of mouth, any channel — bounces before your site has even loaded.

53% bouncebefore the page loads
03

No way to contact you right now

90% of patients expect an immediate response. If you're phone-only during business hours, every patient who searches at night, on a Saturday, or during lunch is booking with whoever they can reach right now. That's not you.

After hoursenquiries go to a competitor
04

Reviews hidden or not there

88% of people trust reviews as much as a personal referral. If a patient lands on your homepage and has to scroll to find social proof — or it isn't there at all — you've lost the trust battle before they've considered calling.

No trustbefore they decide to leave
05

Nothing to do above the fold

Users spend 57% of their time above the fold. If there is no clear, obvious action the moment they land — one button, one CTA, one next step — most visitors leave without contacting you. The page answers no questions and asks for nothing.

57% of timeabove the fold — no CTA
06

Invisible on local Google search

46% of all Google searches are local. Patients searching "cosmetic dentist [city]" who don't see you on page one don't know you exist. They're choosing between the practices who showed up. You're not in the conversation.

Page 2means invisible to new patients
